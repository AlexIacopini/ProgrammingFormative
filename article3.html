Quantum Computers

What are they?

Quantum computers utilise quantum physics in order to create large amounts of computing performance. They differ from 'normal' computers as they do not use transistors and voltages in order to represent ones and zeros. Quantum computation uses quantum bits or qubits which, by using certain mediums, can represent a superposition of a one or a zero state. One qubit can represent a one and a zero simultaneously, meaning lots of data can be handled very quickly. They have huge potential for the amount of processing power they provide, with numerous uses.

Applications of QCs

All the applications of quantum computers are based on the fact the processing capability is much greater than that of a standard transistor computer. Huge amounts of calculations and data management can be dealt with in milliseconds.

Simulation -
In the world of science lots of processes could be simulated in order to save time and money. For example QCs could be used to simulate:
  • The behavior of atoms and particles at unusual conditions such as the reactions inside a collider
  • Structures of proteins and other biological molecules to be used in the development of drugs
  • The compositions of atmospheres on other planets and the possibility finding the correct conditions for life
  • The growth and decline of species' population over thousands of years
  • How certain drugs may affect a person and the interaction it will take with their immune system

Optimisation -
These problems in computer science are some of the most difficult to solve. They generally follow involve trying to find the best possible combination of things whilst limited by some named constraints; there are huge amounts of valid combinations. For example, with just 270 on/off switches, there are more possible combinations than atoms in the universe!

These types of optimisation problems exist in many different domains, such as systems design, traffic flow, mission planning, airline scheduling, financial analysis, web search, and cancer radiotherapy. They are some of the most complex problems in the world, with potentially enormous benefits to businesses, people and science if optimal solutions can be readily computed.
